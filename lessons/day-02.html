Day 2 Number Systems & Exponents

Integer Exponents & Rules

Vocabulary

Base
The number being multiplied repeatedly. In $a^n$, the base is $a$.
Exponent
The small raised number indicating how many times the base is used as a factor. In $a^n$, the exponent is $n$.
Power
The full expression $a^n$, or the result of evaluating it. "$2$ to the fourth power" = $2^4 = 16$.
Zero Exponent
Any non-zero base raised to the 0 equals 1: $a^0 = 1$ (where $a \neq 0$).
Negative Exponent
$a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$. A negative exponent means take the reciprocal — it does not make the result negative.
Scientific Notation
A number written as $a \times 10^n$ where $1 \le |a| < 10$. Used for very large or very small numbers.

Concept

An exponent is shorthand for repeated multiplication. Instead of writing $2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2$, write $2^4$. The diagram below shows the parts of an exponential expression.

$2^4$ = "2 used as a factor 4 times." Base is 2, exponent is 4.

Seven rules cover all integer-exponent simplification. All follow directly from the definition.

PRODUCT RULE
$a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$
Same base → add exponents
QUOTIENT RULE
$\dfrac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}$
Same base → subtract exponents
POWER RULE
$(a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n}$
Power of a power → multiply
POWER OF A PRODUCT
$(ab)^n = a^n b^n$
Distribute to every factor
POWER OF A QUOTIENT
$\!\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^{\!n} = \dfrac{a^n}{b^n}$
Distribute to numerator & denominator
ZERO EXPONENT
$a^0 = 1 \quad (a \neq 0)$
Any nonzero base to the 0 = 1
⚡
Negative Exponent: $a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$
A negative exponent moves the factor to the other level of a fraction. Example: $2^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{8}$.  Also: $\dfrac{1}{x^{-2}} = x^2$.

Scientific notation writes a number as $a \times 10^n$ where $1 \le |a| < 10$. Shift the decimal until exactly one nonzero digit sits to its left. Each shift left increases the exponent by 1; each shift right decreases it by 1.

⚠️
The product and quotient rules only work when the bases are identical. $2^3 \cdot 5^2$ cannot be combined further — different bases.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Combining Rules

Simplify: $\dfrac{x^5 \cdot x^3}{x^4}$

1
Numerator — product rule (same base, add): $x^5 \cdot x^3 = x^{5+3} = x^8$
2
Quotient rule (same base, subtract): $\dfrac{x^8}{x^4} = x^{8-4} = x^4$
$\dfrac{x^5 \cdot x^3}{x^4} = x^4$
Example 2 — Power of a Product

Simplify: $(2x^3)^4$

1
Distribute the exponent 4 to every factor inside: $(2x^3)^4 = 2^4 \cdot (x^3)^4$
2
$2^4 = 16$ and power rule: $(x^3)^4 = x^{3 \times 4} = x^{12}$
$(2x^3)^4 = 16x^{12}$
Example 3 — Negative & Zero Exponents

Simplify: $\dfrac{3^{-2} \cdot 3^5}{3^0}$

1
Zero-exponent rule: $3^0 = 1$, so the denominator is 1.
2
Product rule in the numerator: $3^{-2} \cdot 3^5 = 3^{-2+5} = 3^3$
3
Evaluate: $3^3 = 27$. Dividing by 1 leaves it unchanged.
$\dfrac{3^{-2} \cdot 3^5}{3^0} = 27$

Practice Problems

Simplify $x^3 \cdot x^5$.

Same base — product rule. Do you add or multiply the exponents?
Product rule: $x^3 \cdot x^5 = x^{3+5} = x^8$. The coefficient stays 1 — don't write $2x^8$.

Simplify $\dfrac{y^7}{y^3}$.

Quotient rule: same base in a fraction — subtract (numerator exponent minus denominator exponent).
$\dfrac{y^7}{y^3} = y^{7-3} = y^4$.

Evaluate $5^0$.

Any non-zero base to the power of zero equals…
$5^0 = 1$. In general $a^0 = 1$ for $a \neq 0$. Verify: $\dfrac{5^3}{5^3} = 5^{3-3} = 5^0$, and any number over itself is 1.

Evaluate $2^{-3}$. Enter as a fraction (e.g. 1/8) or decimal.

$a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$. First find $2^3$, then take its reciprocal.
$2^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{2^3} = \dfrac{1}{8}$. A negative exponent does not make the answer negative.

Simplify $(x^2)^5$.

Power rule: a power raised to another power — do you add or multiply the exponents?
Power rule: $(x^2)^5 = x^{2 \times 5} = x^{10}$. Multiply the exponents.

Simplify $\dfrac{a^8}{a^8}$ (assume $a \neq 0$).

Try the quotient rule: subtract the exponents. What does $a^0$ equal?
$\dfrac{a^8}{a^8} = a^{8-8} = a^0 = 1$. This shows exactly why the zero-exponent rule must be true.

Which expression is equivalent to $x^{-4}$?

$a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$. The negative exponent means reciprocal — not a negative value.
$x^{-4} = \dfrac{1}{x^4}$. The negative exponent moves the factor to the denominator; it does not negate $x$.

Which correctly expresses $340{,}000$ in scientific notation?

Scientific notation requires $1 \le |a| < 10$. How many places do you move the decimal in 340,000?
Move the decimal 5 places left: $340000 \to 3.4$, so exponent = 5. Answer: $3.4 \times 10^5$.
A and C fail the $1 \le a < 10$ rule. D gives the wrong value: $3.4 \times 10^4 = 34{,}000$.

Simplify $(3x^2y)^3$.

Raise every factor to the 3rd power. Don't forget: $3^3 = ?$
$(3x^2y)^3 = 3^3 \cdot (x^2)^3 \cdot y^3 = 27 \cdot x^{6} \cdot y^3 = 27x^6y^3$.
A common mistake is forgetting to cube the coefficient 3, leaving it as just 3.

Simplify $\dfrac{(2x)^3 \cdot x^2}{4x^4}$.

Expand $(2x)^3$ first, then multiply in the numerator, then divide.
$(2x)^3 = 8x^3$  →  numerator: $8x^3 \cdot x^2 = 8x^5$
$\dfrac{8x^5}{4x^4} = \dfrac{8}{4} \cdot x^{5-4} = 2x^1 = 2x$
