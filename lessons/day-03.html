Day 3 Number Systems & Exponents

Radicals & Rational Exponents

Vocabulary

Radical
The expression $\sqrt[n]{a}$, meaning "the $n$th root of $a$." The symbol $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$ is the radical sign.
Radicand
The expression under the radical sign. In $\sqrt{50}$, the radicand is 50.
Index
The small number indicating which root to take. $\sqrt[3]{8}$ has index 3. When no index is written, the index is 2 (square root).
Principal Root
The non-negative square root. $\sqrt{9} = 3$, not $-3$. (Both $3$ and $-3$ square to $9$, but the principal root is the positive one.)
Simplest Radical Form
A radical is simplified when: (1) no perfect-square factor remains under the radical, (2) no fractions appear under the radical, and (3) no radical appears in the denominator.
Rational Exponent
An exponent written as a fraction: $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$ and $a^{m/n} = \sqrt[n]{a^m} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m$.

Concept

The square root of $a$ is the number that, when squared, equals $a$. The cube root of $a$ is the number that, when cubed, equals $a$. Unlike square roots, cube roots can be negative: $\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2$ because $(-2)^3 = -8$.

Simplifying a square radical means pulling out any perfect-square factors from under the sign. The diagram below shows the process for $\sqrt{72}$.

📐
Two rules for radical arithmetic:
Product rule: $\sqrt[n]{a} \cdot \sqrt[n]{b} = \sqrt[n]{ab}$  ·  Quotient rule: $\dfrac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}} = \sqrt[n]{\dfrac{a}{b}}$   (both require $a, b \ge 0$)
You can only combine radicals with the same index.

Rational exponents are a bridge between exponents and radicals. The denominator of the fraction is the root index; the numerator is the power:

$$a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a} \qquad a^{m/n} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m = \sqrt[n]{a^m}$$

Example: $8^{2/3} = \left(\sqrt[3]{8}\right)^2 = 2^2 = 4$. Always take the root first (when possible) — it keeps the numbers small.

⚠️
$\sqrt{a + b} \neq \sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b}$. For example, $\sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$, but $\sqrt{9} + \sqrt{16} = 3 + 4 = 7 \neq 5$.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Simplify a Radical

Simplify $\sqrt{48}$.

1
Find the largest perfect-square factor of 48. Factors of 48: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 16, 24, 48. The largest perfect square is 16.
2
Rewrite: $\sqrt{48} = \sqrt{16 \cdot 3}$
3
Product rule: $\sqrt{16 \cdot 3} = \sqrt{16} \cdot \sqrt{3} = 4\sqrt{3}$
$\sqrt{48} = 4\sqrt{3}$
Example 2 — Cube Root (Negative)

Simplify $\sqrt[3]{-27}$.

1
Ask: what number, when cubed, gives $-27$?
2
$(-3)^3 = (-3)(-3)(-3) = 9 \cdot (-3) = -27$ ✓
$\sqrt[3]{-27} = -3$
Example 3 — Rational Exponents

Evaluate $8^{2/3}$.

1
The denominator 3 means cube root; the numerator 2 means square: $8^{2/3} = \left(\sqrt[3]{8}\right)^2$
2
Cube root first (keeps numbers small): $\sqrt[3]{8} = 2$ because $2^3 = 8$
3
Then square: $2^2 = 4$
$8^{2/3} = 4$

Practice Problems

Simplify $\sqrt{100}$.

What number times itself equals 100?
$\sqrt{100} = 10$ because $10^2 = 100$. We take the principal (positive) root.

Simplify $\sqrt{75}$.

What is the largest perfect-square factor of 75? (Think: 75 = ? × ?)
$75 = 25 \cdot 3$ and 25 is a perfect square ($5^2$). So $\sqrt{75} = \sqrt{25 \cdot 3} = \sqrt{25}\cdot\sqrt{3} = 5\sqrt{3}$.

Simplify $\sqrt[3]{-64}$.

What number, when multiplied by itself three times, gives $-64$? (Cube roots of negatives are negative.)
$(-4)^3 = (-4)(-4)(-4) = 16 \cdot (-4) = -64$. So $\sqrt[3]{-64} = -4$. Cube roots can be negative; square roots cannot.

Which expression is equivalent to $x^{1/2}$?

$a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$. What is the index when the denominator is 2?
$x^{1/2} = \sqrt[2]{x} = \sqrt{x}$. The denominator of the rational exponent is the index of the radical.

Evaluate $27^{1/3}$.

$a^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{a}$. What number cubed gives 27?
$27^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{27} = 3$ because $3^3 = 27$.

Simplify $\sqrt{18} \cdot \sqrt{2}$.

Use the product rule: $\sqrt{a} \cdot \sqrt{b} = \sqrt{ab}$. Then simplify.
$\sqrt{18} \cdot \sqrt{2} = \sqrt{18 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{36} = 6$.

Evaluate $8^{2/3}$.

$a^{m/n} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m$. Take the cube root of 8 first, then square it.
$8^{2/3} = \left(\sqrt[3]{8}\right)^2 = 2^2 = 4$.

Simplify $\dfrac{\sqrt{50}}{\sqrt{2}}$.

Quotient rule: $\dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}$. What is $\dfrac{50}{2}$?
$\dfrac{\sqrt{50}}{\sqrt{2}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{50}{2}} = \sqrt{25} = 5$.

Which expression is already in simplest radical form?

Check each radicand for perfect-square factors greater than 1. If any exist, the radical can still be simplified.
$\sqrt{12} = 2\sqrt{3}$ (12 has factor 4). $\sqrt{45} = 3\sqrt{5}$ (45 has factor 9). $\sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}$ (8 has factor 4).
$3\sqrt{5}$: the radicand 5 has no perfect-square factors other than 1. ✓ Already simplified.

Rewrite $\sqrt[5]{x^3}$ using a rational exponent.

$\sqrt[n]{a^m} = a^{m/n}$. What is the index? What is the power inside?
$\sqrt[5]{x^3} = x^{3/5}$. The index (5) becomes the denominator; the power of the radicand (3) becomes the numerator.
